# Long-Term Positioning (3-5 Years)

**Purpose**: Strategic career trajectory mapping beyond immediate opportunities.  
**Last Updated**: January 15, 2026  
**Audience**: Self (long-term planning)

---

## Core Principle: Career Is Series of Choices, Not Destiny

**Not**: "I'll become a principal engineer."

**Why**: Treats career as single path. Ignores optionality and changing preferences.

---

**Is**: "In 3-5 years, I want these options available: IC track, Staff/Principal track, Consulting, or Technical Leadership. I'll build capabilities that keep all paths open, then choose based on what I value at that time."

**Why**: Preserves optionality. Career preferences change. Build leverage, not commitment.

---

## The Options (3-5 Year Paths)

### Option 1: Senior IC (Individual Contributor)

**What It Is**: You remain hands-on technical. You own systems, write code, design architecture. No management responsibilities.

**Day-to-Day**:

- Design and implement complex systems
- Deep technical work (chaos testing, architectural decisions, operational planning)
- Mentor junior engineers informally
- Collaborate with team on technical direction

**Compensation Range**: $140K-180K salary, or $100-130/hour contract

**Appeal**:

- Stay technical (no management meetings)
- Deep work focus (flow states, building)
- Clear output (you ship systems)
- Work-life boundary (clock out when done)

**Trade-Offs**:

- Limited scope (you own your system, not company direction)
- Compensation ceiling (caps around $180K-200K for IC roles)
- Less influence (you advise, don't decide company strategy)

**Rachel Foods Fit**: Strong. Rachel Foods demonstrates IC capabilities—design, testing, documentation.

---

### Option 2: Staff/Principal Engineer

**What It Is**: Senior technical leadership without direct reports. You own technical direction across multiple systems or company-wide.

**Day-to-Day**:

- Architectural decisions spanning multiple teams
- Technical strategy (which problems to solve, how to solve them)
- Mentoring senior engineers
- Writing code occasionally (design, prototypes, critical systems)
- Cross-functional collaboration (product, executive team)

**Compensation Range**: $180K-250K+ salary, or $130-200+/hour contract

**Appeal**:

- High technical influence (you shape company technical direction)
- Variety (work across systems, not just one)
- Mentor impact (help senior engineers grow)
- Compensation ceiling is higher (can reach $300K+ at large companies)

**Trade-Offs**:

- Less hands-on (more design, less implementation)
- More meetings (coordination across teams)
- Politics (navigating organizational dynamics)
- Pressure (technical failures are your responsibility)

**Rachel Foods Fit**: Strong. Rachel Foods demonstrates judgment required—documented decisions, trade-off analysis, operational planning.

---

### Option 3: Engineering Management

**What It Is**: Lead team of engineers. You're responsible for hiring, performance management, team delivery, and people development.

**Day-to-Day**:

- 1:1s with direct reports
- Hiring interviews and onboarding
- Performance reviews and career development
- Team process optimization
- Stakeholder communication (product, executive)
- Coding: 0-20% of time (mostly code review, unblocking team)

**Compensation Range**: $150K-220K+ salary

**Appeal**:

- People impact (help engineers grow, build high-performing teams)
- Organizational influence (shape team culture, processes)
- Leadership skills (communication, delegation, conflict resolution)
- Career progression (Director → VP → CTO path)

**Trade-Offs**:

- Limited hands-on technical work (you enable others, not build yourself)
- People problems (conflict, underperformance, turnover)
- Meeting-heavy (little deep work time)
- On-call for people issues (late-night message from stressed engineer)

**Rachel Foods Fit**: Weak. Rachel Foods demonstrates technical depth, not management capability. Would need separate evidence (team leadership, mentoring, process improvement).

**Recommendation**: Only pursue if you genuinely want to develop people and build teams. Don't default into management because "it's the next step."

---

### Option 4: Technical Consulting

**What It Is**: Independent consultant. Clients hire you for specific expertise (architecture design, system audit, failure prevention, technical due diligence).

**Day-to-Day**:

- Short-term engagements (2 weeks - 6 months)
- Problem-solving work (diagnose issues, design solutions, sometimes implement)
- Client communication (explaining technical decisions to non-technical stakeholders)
- Business development (finding clients, proposals, contracts)
- Flexibility (choose projects, set schedule)

**Compensation Range**: $130-250+/hour, varies by engagement

**Appeal**:

- Maximum autonomy (you choose clients, projects, schedule)
- Variety (different industries, problems, companies)
- High earning potential (good consultants make $200K-500K+)
- Learning (each project exposes you to new domains)

**Trade-Offs**:

- Income volatility (gaps between projects)
- Business overhead (proposals, contracts, invoicing, taxes)
- No team (solo work, less collaboration)
- Always selling (even between projects, you're finding next client)

**Rachel Foods Fit**: Very strong. Rachel Foods is portfolio piece. Documentation demonstrates consulting deliverables (decisions, trade-offs, operational planning).

**Trajectory**: 2-3 more projects like Rachel Foods → consulting is viable path.

---

### Option 5: Technical Product/Startup

**What It Is**: Join early-stage startup as technical co-founder or early engineer. You build product, own technical direction, and have equity upside.

**Day-to-Day**:

- Full-stack ownership (you build everything early on)
- Product decisions (what to build, not just how)
- Hiring (as company grows, you build team)
- Firefighting (startups are chaotic, expect constant pivots)

**Compensation Range**: Below-market salary ($80K-120K) + equity (0.5%-10% depending on stage)

**Appeal**:

- Equity upside (if startup succeeds, equity worth $X00K-$XM+)
- Product ownership (you shape what gets built)
- Learning velocity (intense, fast-paced environment)
- Mission (if you care about startup's problem)

**Trade-Offs**:

- High risk (90% of startups fail, equity often worth $0)
- Low salary (below market for 2-5+ years)
- Long hours (expectation of hustle, boundaries are weak)
- Chaos (pivots, layoffs, unclear direction)

**Rachel Foods Fit**: Medium. Rachel Foods demonstrates you can ship systems, but startups care more about speed than rigor. Your chaos testing might be seen as "over-engineering" in early-stage startup.

**Recommendation**: Only pursue if: (1) you believe in startup's mission deeply, (2) you're okay with high risk, (3) you can afford below-market salary for 2-5 years.

## Success Beyond Money

### What Is Success?

**Not**: Maximizing income at all costs.

**Why**: High income with burnout, poor health, or misery isn't success.

---

**Is**: Sustainable career where you enjoy the work, maintain health/relationships, and feel competent.

**How to Measure**:

1. **Enjoyment**: Do you look forward to Mondays? Or dread them?
2. **Health**: Are you sleeping well? Exercising? Low stress?
3. **Relationships**: Do you have time for family/friends? Or is work consuming everything?
4. **Competence**: Do you feel like you're growing? Or stagnating?
5. **Autonomy**: Do you have control over your work? Or are you constantly overridden?

**Rachel Foods Context**: You built Rachel Foods because you enjoyed it. That's success signal. If professional work stops being enjoyable, something is wrong.

---

### Metrics That Matter (Beyond Compensation)

**Metric #1: Projects You're Proud Of**

**Question**: In last year, how many projects would you enthusiastically show someone?

**Target**: 2-3 per year.

**Why**: If you're not proud of your work, compensation doesn't matter. You're compromising.

---

**Metric #2: Learning Velocity**

**Question**: Are you learning new skills/domains? Or repeating same work?

**Target**: Learn 1-2 new domains or skills per year.

**Why**: Stagnation leads to boredom. Learning keeps work interesting.

---

**Metric #3: Boundary Respect**

**Question**: Do clients/employers respect your time? Or constant interruptions/weekend work?

**Target**: 95%+ of time, boundaries are respected.

**Why**: Disrespect of boundaries leads to burnout. Sustainable career requires boundaries.

---

**Metric #4: Decision Authority**

**Question**: Do you have authority over technical decisions? Or constantly overridden?

**Target**: 80%+ of technical decisions are yours.

**Why**: If you can't make decisions, you're not using your judgment. That's frustrating.

---

**Metric #5: Stress Level**

**Question**: Do you feel calm most days? Or constant anxiety?

**Target**: 80%+ of days, stress is manageable.

**Why**: Chronic stress damages health. Not worth any compensation.

---

### When to Reprioritize

**Scenario #1: High Income, High Stress**

**Example**: Consulting at $250/hour, but working 60-hour weeks, constant client demands, no boundaries.

**Decision**: Scale back. Take fewer clients. Prioritize health over income.

**Why**: Money is renewable. Health isn't.

---

**Scenario #2: Comfortable Income, Stagnation**

**Example**: Senior IC at $160K, but repeating same work for 3 years. Bored.

**Decision**: Take learning opportunity even if income drops slightly. Or take side project to learn new domain.

**Why**: Stagnation leads to career decline. Learning keeps you valuable.

---

**Scenario #3: Good Work, Toxic Environment**

**Example**: Interesting technical work, but toxic manager or blame culture.

**Decision**: Exit. Find better environment even if work is less interesting.

**Why**: Toxic environments damage mental health. Not sustainable.

## Work to Permanently Avoid

### Category 1: Low-Stakes, Low-Learning Work

**Examples**:

- Marketing websites
- CRUD applications with no complexity
- Internal tools with no technical challenge

**Why Avoid**: No portfolio value, no learning, no positioning benefit.

**Exception**: If compensation is extremely high ($150+/hour), short-term acceptable. But don't make it your focus.

---

### Category 2: Pure Execution Roles with No Authority

**Examples**:

- Agency work implementing client specs with no input
- Large company roles where senior architect makes all decisions
- Offshore team augmentation (you're just extra hands)

**Why Avoid**: No decision authority = can't demonstrate judgment = weak portfolio = career stagnation.

**Exception**: If you're early in career and need stable income, acceptable short-term. But plan exit within 1-2 years.

---

### Category 3: Toxic Cultures

**Examples**:

- Blame-focused (failures are always someone's fault)
- Micromanagement (constant surveillance, no trust)
- Unrealistic expectations (ship perfect system in 2 weeks)

**Why Avoid**: Damages mental health, burnout risk, no sustainable.

**Exception**: None. Exit immediately if you identify these patterns.

---

### Category 4: "Passionate Hustler" Roles

**Examples**:

- Startups looking for "rockstar ninja who loves coding 24/7"
- Roles with unlimited scope ("we all wear many hats")
- Equity-only or below-market salary with vague promises

**Why Avoid**: Exploitation masked as opportunity. Unlimited expectations, limited compensation, weak boundaries.

**Exception**: Only if you deeply believe in mission AND can afford below-market salary AND have explicit equity agreement with real value.

---

### Category 5: Roles That Reset Your Positioning

**Examples**:

- Frontend work when you're positioning as backend specialist
- Generalist roles when you're building depth
- Low-quality clients who don't value rigor

**Why Avoid**: Dilutes positioning. Every project should reinforce your moat, not reset it.

**Exception**: If compensation is 2x+ your rate, short-term acceptable. But don't let it define you.

## Optionality Preservation Strategy

### The Goal

**Not**: Lock into one path now.

**Why**: Preferences change. Market changes. Locking in limits options.

---

**Is**: Build capabilities that keep multiple paths open. Decide later based on what you value at that time.

**How**: Do work that transfers across paths.

---

### Capabilities That Preserve Optionality

**Capability #1: Systems Thinking**

**Transfers To**:

- Senior IC (you design systems)
- Staff/Principal (you design across multiple systems)
- Consulting (clients hire you for architecture design)

**How to Build**: Do work like Rachel Foods. Understand component interactions, failure modes, trade-offs.

---

**Capability #2: Documented Decision-Making**

**Transfers To**:

- Senior IC (you document your decisions)
- Staff/Principal (you explain decisions to stakeholders)
- Consulting (deliverable is often decision documentation)

**How to Build**: Write ARCHITECTURE_DECISIONS.md for every project. Practice explaining rationale.

---

**Capability #3: Proactive Failure Prevention**

**Transfers To**:

- Senior IC (you test your systems rigorously)
- Staff/Principal (you define testing standards)
- Consulting (clients hire you to prevent failures)

**How to Build**: Write chaos tests. Find failures before production. Build reputation for reliability.

---

**Capability #4: Communication**

**Transfers To**:

- Senior IC (you explain technical decisions to team)
- Staff/Principal (you explain strategy to executives)
- Consulting (you explain recommendations to clients)
- Management (you communicate with direct reports)

**How to Build**: Practice translating technical to business language. Write clear documentation. Present your work.

---

### Capabilities That Lock You Into One Path

**Capability: Deep Tool Expertise**

**Example**: Becoming "the Kubernetes expert."

**Risk**: If Kubernetes declines or company doesn't use it, your value drops. Locked into one tool.

**Mitigation**: Build depth in principles (orchestration, deployment, scaling), not just tools.

---

**Capability: Company-Specific Knowledge**

**Example**: Becoming expert in one company's internal systems.

**Risk**: Knowledge doesn't transfer. When you leave, you're starting from scratch.

**Mitigation**: Generalize learnings. Document patterns that transfer ("This company does X, but principle applies to Y").

---

**Capability: Management Without Technical Depth**

**Example**: Becoming manager without maintaining technical skills.

**Risk**: If you want to return to IC work, you're out of date. Locked into management.

**Mitigation**: Stay hands-on even as manager (code reviews, architecture, prototypes).

## Decision-Making Rules

### Rule #1: Optimize for Learning in First 5 Years

**Rationale**: Skills compound. Learn fast early, then leverage later.

**Application**: Accept projects that stretch you, even if compensation is slightly lower. Avoid repetitive work even if compensation is higher.

---

### Rule #2: Optimize for Compensation After You Have Depth

**Rationale**: Once you have moat (depth + reputation), maximize earnings.

**Application**: After 5-7 years, be selective. Accept only high-compensation or high-learning work. Decline everything else.

---

### Rule #3: Never Sacrifice Health for Income

**Rationale**: Health is non-renewable. Income is renewable.

**Application**: If stress is chronic, exit. No amount of money justifies burnout.

---

### Rule #4: Preserve Optionality Until You're Sure

**Rationale**: Locking into one path early is risky. Preferences change.

**Application**: Do work that transfers across paths (systems thinking, communication, documented decisions). Avoid work that locks you in (company-specific knowledge, single-tool expertise).

---

### Rule #5: Avoid Reset-to-Zero Work

**Rationale**: Career compounds. Work that doesn't compound is waste.

**Application**: Every project should either (1) build moat, (2) pay extremely well, or (3) both. Decline work that does neither.

- If yes: Continue.
- If no: Find new challenges or exit.

---

**Q2**: Am I proud of my work?

---

**Q3**: Am I compensated fairly?

- If yes: Continue.
- If no: Negotiate raise or find better opportunity.

---

**Q4**: Do I have boundaries?

- If yes: Continue.
- If no: Set boundaries or exit.

---

**Q5**: Do I have optionality?

- If yes: Continue.
- If no: Build capabilities that transfer across paths.

---

**Q6**: What path am I gravitating toward?

- IC? Staff/Principal? Consulting? Management? Startup?
- Why? (Be honest with yourself)
- Is current work aligned with that path?

---

**Long-Term Thinking**: "I'll build capabilities that open options. When I'm 30, I'll choose based on what I value then."

**Why It's Better**: Removes pressure. Preserves optionality. Allows for changing preferences.

---

**Rachel Foods Context**: You built Rachel Foods without external pressure. You chose rigor over speed, depth over breadth. That's long-term thinking.

**Trajectory**: Continue building capabilities (systems thinking, documented decisions, proactive testing). Paths will open naturally. Choose when you're ready, not because you're rushed.
